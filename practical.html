Practical Information

Everything you need to know before you go — visas, money, safety, and more

Entry & Visas

Switzerland is part of the Schengen Area but is not an EU member. Citizens of EU/EEA countries and many other nations (including the US, UK, Canada, Australia, Japan) can enter Switzerland visa-free for up to 90 days within a 180-day period. Nationals of other countries should check with their nearest Swiss embassy. A valid passport is required — some EU citizens can use national ID cards.

Money & Currency

  • Currency: Swiss Franc (CHF). 1 CHF ≈ 1.13 USD / 0.88 GBP (approximate)
  • Euros accepted: Sometimes in tourist areas, but you'll receive change in CHF at a poor rate — use CHF
  • ATMs (Bancomats): Widely available and the best way to get local currency
  • Cards: Visa and Mastercard accepted almost everywhere; Amex less so in smaller places
  • Tipping: Not mandatory — service is included in Swiss prices. Rounding up the bill is common and appreciated
  • Contactless payments: Widely accepted including Apple/Google Pay

Safety

Switzerland is one of the safest countries in the world. Violent crime is extremely rare, infrastructure is excellent, and emergency services are first-class. Standard urban precautions apply — watch for pickpockets in tourist-heavy areas like Zurich's main station. In the mountains, always check weather conditions and respect altitude warnings.

Language

Switzerland has four official languages distributed by region. German is spoken by about 63% of the population (north, east, and centre), French by 23% (west — called Romandy), Italian by 8% (canton Ticino), and Romansh by less than 1% (parts of Graubünden). English is very widely spoken throughout the country, especially in tourism, business, and cities.

Health & Insurance

  • Healthcare quality: Among the best in the world — excellent hospitals in all major cities
  • EHIC/GHIC: EU/UK health cards give access to state healthcare but Switzerland is expensive — travel insurance is strongly recommended
  • Pharmacies (Apotheke/Pharmacie): Well-stocked and pharmacists are knowledgeable. Many can treat minor conditions
  • Altitude sickness: Possible above 2,500m — ascend gradually, stay hydrated, descend if symptoms worsen
  • Tap water: Completely safe to drink everywhere in Switzerland

Electricity & Internet

Electricity

Voltage: 230V / 50Hz

Plug type: Type J (unique Swiss 3-pin). EU Type C plugs also work. UK/US travellers need adaptors.

nternet & SIM

Free Wi-Fi widely available in hotels, cafes, and train stations. Swiss SIM cards are easy to purchase at airports and supermarkets (Sunrise, Salt, Swisscom). Roaming from EU countries is generally fine.
