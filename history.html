The Founding of Switzerland

The traditional founding date is 1 August 1291, when representatives of the three forest cantons of Uri, Schwyz, and Unterwalden met at the Rütli meadow above Lake Lucerne and signed the Federal Charter. This oath of mutual assistance is celebrated as Swiss National Day every August 1st with fireworks and bonfires across the country.

🗡️ William Tell

The legendary Swiss folk hero, William Tell, is said to have shot an apple off his son's head after refusing to bow to the Habsburg governor Gessler. Whether historical fact or myth, Tell remains the enduring symbol of Swiss resistance to tyranny and the spirit of freedom that shaped the nation.

Swiss Neutrality

Switzerland has maintained armed neutrality since 1515, when defeat at Marignano taught Swiss leaders the limits of military expansion. This neutrality has made Switzerland a globally trusted host for international organisations, peace negotiations, and humanitarian missions. Geneva in particular is home to hundreds of international bodies including the United Nations and the International Red Cross.

Historic Sites to Visit

  • Rütli Meadow – The symbolic birthplace of Switzerland, accessible only by boat on Lake Lucerne
  • Chillon Castle – A perfectly preserved medieval lakeside fortress near Montreux
  • Bern Old Town – UNESCO Heritage medieval city, largely unchanged since the 15th century
  • Hallstatt & Laténe – Archaeological sites that give their names to Iron Age Celtic cultures
  • Augusta Raurica – The best-preserved Roman site in the Alpine region, near Basel
  • Tell's Chapel – A small chapel on Lake Lucerne marking Tell's legendary leap to freedom
